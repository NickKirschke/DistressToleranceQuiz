Identifying Your Triggers & Building Capacity

Distress, the discomfort, pain, or difficulty we feel when something is hard, is a normal and expected part of being human. Distress tolerance, the capacity to experience uncomfortable emotions or difficult situations without shutting down, withdrawing, or responding impulsively, is a skill that can be built over time.

Distress tolerance is about how you behave when you feel distressed, rather than how distressing the situation feels. Whether the distress is intense or mild matters less than what you do with it.

Life is hard. We experience unpleasant, unwanted situations, dynamics, and feelings all the time. This exercise is not about eliminating distress, that is neither possible nor desirable. It is about learning to recognize it, understand it, and develop the capacity to remain present and make thoughtful, values-based choices even when it's hard.

This exercise is not about judging how you respond to difficulty. Nothing you select is "bad" or "wrong." Think of this as collecting data about yourself. This is about understanding yourself more clearly so you can move through hard things with more intention.

You will be shown 50 situations one at a time. For each one, rate how easy or hard it typically is for you to navigate. It takes about 10 minutes.

Part 5

Building Distress Tolerance

The following strategies are drawn from dialectical behavior therapy (DBT), acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT), and research on emotional regulation. No single strategy works for everyone. The goal is to find what fits best for you, practice it in low-stakes situations, and build from there.

Shift Your Relationship With Discomfort
  • Remind yourself: "This is uncomfortable, not dangerous."
  • Recognize that tolerating distress in small doses builds capacity over time.
  • Practice self-compassion rather than self-criticism: how you speak to yourself in distress matters.
  • Normalize discomfort as a natural part of growth, risk, and being in relationship with others.
Create Space Before Reacting
DBT: STOP Skill
  • Stop, pause before responding when emotions are activated
  • Take a slow breath, physiological regulation precedes cognitive clarity
  • Observe what you are feeling and what is actually happening vs. what you fear
  • Proceed with intention, act from your values, not from the urgency of the feeling
Ground Yourself in the Present
DBT: Grounding & ACT: Present-Moment Awareness
  • Use your senses to anchor yourself: name 5 things you can see, 4 you can hear, 3 you can touch
  • Name the feeling directly: "I am feeling anxious", labeling reduces its intensity
  • Focus on what is concretely true right now, rather than projected fears or worst-case scenarios
Approach Rather Than Avoid
Behavioral Exposure Principles
  • Avoidance provides short-term relief but strengthens distress over time
  • Gradually approach situations that activate moderate discomfort, start small and build
  • Stick with a hard task even briefly past the point of wanting to stop, this builds tolerance directly
  • Break overwhelming situations into the smallest possible next step
Regulate Your Baseline
DBT: PLEASE Skills

Your ability to tolerate distress is directly affected by how well you are taking care of your body. When these basics are neglected, your tolerance threshold lowers significantly.

LetterStands ForWhy It Matters
PLTreat Physical IllnessUntreated physical discomfort raises your emotional reactivity baseline. Physical health and emotional regulation are not separate.
EBalanced EatingIrregular nutrition directly affects mood stability and your capacity to tolerate frustration. Not about eating perfectly, about eating consistently.
AAvoid Mood-Altering SubstancesAlcohol, cannabis, and other substances offer short-term relief but reduce regulation capacity over time and can make triggers feel more intense, not less.
SBalanced SleepSleep deprivation is one of the most reliable ways to lower distress tolerance. Consistent, sufficient sleep is among the highest-leverage actions you can take.
EExercise / MovementMoving your body, even a short walk, has a measurable impact on mood, stress, and anxiety. You need consistent movement, not an intense workout.
  • Step away from a distressing situation briefly when needed, with a commitment to return
Communicate Directly
ACT: Values-Based Action & DBT: Interpersonal Effectiveness
  • Ask for clarification before assuming negative intent
  • Express needs clearly and specifically rather than hoping others will infer them
  • Practice setting small, low-stakes limits, boundary-setting becomes easier with repetition
Reflect After Difficult Moments
DBT: Chain Analysis & ACT: Self-as-Context
  • Ask: What did I feel? What triggered it? What did I do? What would I try differently?
  • Notice, without judgment, where you handled something better than you would have before
  • Growth in distress tolerance is rarely dramatic, track the small shifts
Set a Focused Goal
  • Identify one specific trigger area you want to build tolerance for
  • Choose one concrete, small way to practice it this week
  • Track progress, even incremental progress, as meaningful data
